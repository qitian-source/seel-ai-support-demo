Seel Compass — Product Requirements

A merchant-facing tool that gives an objective, structured baseline assessment of common go-global gaps. Points to relevant rules, recommends counsel review, and never renders a legal conclusion.

Product: Seel Compass · Go-Global Readiness

5. Legal safeguards ⭐

5.1 Language & framing rules

  • Never used: violates, illegal, non-compliant, deceptive, unlawful, you must …
  • Always used for review items: Review item: … Recommend confirming with qualified counsel whether …
  • Persistent footer on every report tab: "Informational only — not legal advice, and not a law firm. Review items point to relevant rules and recommend confirming with qualified counsel."
  • Category-specific scope disclaimers in-context (IP tab: "name-only, not FTO"; Entity: "informational only, confirm with counsel or tax advisor")
  • Intro disclaimer (counsel-drafted, Michael 7/8) — shown on the first onboarding screen, before any scan runs, verbatim: "This scan is an automated, informational tool that detects whether certain signals appear on the page you've identified as your own. It is not legal advice or a determination of compliance, and creates no attorney-client relationship. Results are not exhaustive and may contain errors. Consult your own attorney on any compliance question. Seel disclaims all warranties and liability arising from use of the scan." (Note: this counsel-drafted text uses "compliance" in the disclaiming sense — "not a determination of compliance." To reconcile with the 6/18 direction to drop "compliance" from outward product language, confirm with Jenny; recommendation is to keep counsel's disclaimer wording as-is.)

5.2 Self-harm handling — scope out, don't suppress (revised per Michael 7/8)

An agent trained to detect risk signals in merchant operations will, by default, flag Seel's own product model where it is embedded on the merchant's page. v1.2 handles this at corpus-authoring time, not at scan time. Earlier drafts ran every rule and then suppressed the finding on Seel components (an "omit-not-label" whitelist). Counsel's guidance (7/8): running a rule everywhere and then selectively removing its result on Seel's own product is an affirmative act that could be characterized as misleading. So the whitelist is removed.
Invariant — only two states, no third: a rule is either in scope (it runs uniformly for every merchant and its findings belong to the merchant) or out of scope (it does not exist in the methodology and is checked for no one). There is no "runs-but-hidden" state. Nothing is suppressed at scan time.
Rule typeExamplesTreatment
Exists only to catch the pattern Seel's product uses Default-enabled add-on disclosure · opt-out-flow visibility · shipping-insurance fee display Removed from the corpus entirely. Checked for no merchant. Same logic as the checkout-flow analysis already removed on 6/18 — these live in the checkout surface that is out of scope.
General rule that may incidentally touch a Seel component General cookie-consent rule firing on a cookie set by an embedded Seel widget Runs normally, no Seel-specific handling. The finding belongs to the merchant — it is their page and their embedded choice. No suppression.
  • Consequence: Compass will not surface default-enabled-add-on / opt-out / fee-display review items for any merchant. This is an accepted, uniform scoping decision — not a Seel-specific one.
  • The fingerprint-detection + skip-whitelist + suppression machinery (former State Machine B) is deleted. No audit log of suppressions is needed because no suppression occurs.
  • Checkout flow remains not crawled at all (unchanged) — no paper trail of analysis exists.
  • Open for counsel confirmation: (a) that the removed set above is the complete list of "scope-out" rules; (b) that when a general in-scope rule fires on a page containing a Seel component, surfacing the finding as the merchant's page behavior (evidence may reference the Seel-set cookie/copy) is the correct treatment. See §12 item 2.

5.3 Counsel involvement model — middle path (proposed)

Needs counsel sign-off. See State Machine C (§6.3).
  1. One-time sign-off on the methodology + regulatory corpus (public text of GDPR / CCPA / state privacy laws / FTC Act §5 / Mag-Moss / FTC Green Guides / etc. as ingested).
  2. Re-sign on material change (new market, new category, new corpus version). Turnaround target: ≤ 5 business days.
  3. First N reports (proposed N = 5) per new category or new market reviewed before that category/market goes live to merchants.
  4. Steady state: ~5% random spot-check + auto-route of any "high-severity" finding into a counsel review queue. Counsel decides batch-approve or individual review.

Why the middle path: per-finding sign-off (Alex 6/9 view) recreates the $50–100k/market counsel cost Compass exists to reduce. Once-and-done sign-off (Zack 6/9 view) leaves category-specific drift unhandled. The middle path bounds counsel cost while catching drift.

5.4 Scan authorization & domain-ownership verification (new in v1.1)

What this prevents: without verification, anyone could request a Compass report on any domain — including a competitor's — and Seel would be generating a structured document of "review items" about a third party who never consented. That is exactly the paper-trail exposure raised on 6/11. Two independent controls close it:
ControlMechanismWhat it protects
Authorization affirmation Required checkbox at onboarding: "I confirm I am authorized to have this domain reviewed." Cannot proceed without it. Timestamped and retained. Contractual layer — the requesting user takes on the representation
Domain-ownership verification DNS TXT record challenge (seel-compass-verify=…), industry-standard (same pattern as Google Search Console / SSL issuance). Scans only run against verified domains. Diligence layer — Seel has independently confirmed control of the domain, not merely accepted a claim

Phase 1 is invite-only with manually vetted merchants, so the practical marginal risk is low — but the control is in the product from day one so the GA path does not depend on retrofitting it, and so this PRD can represent that no unverified domain is ever scanned.

5.5 Audit log — internal only

  • Corpus version + LLM/prompt version applied to each scan
  • Category-detection outcomes vs merchant declarations
  • Any LLM-vs-merchant conflict (for future audit if a merchant claims a category doesn't apply when the agent detected it)

Note (v1.2): the former "fingerprint matches" and "skip-rule applications" entries are gone — with suppression removed (§5.2), there is nothing of that kind to log.

Retention: engagement length + 3 years. Not surfaced to merchant. Available to counsel on request.

5.6 Source & proof layer (new in v1.2)

Every finding surfaces two distinct layers, and the distinction is itself a legal safeguard: citations attach to rules, never to conclusions.

LayerAnswersContentsProvenance
Source "What rule does this check derive from?" Framework + article citation chips linking to official primary texts only (EUR-Lex, eCFR, ftc.gov, leginfo, USPTO, State SoS) Rule registry — versioned with the corpus, counsel-signed (SM C)
Proof "What did the scan observe on my site?" Pages checked + timestamp · verbatim excerpts ("captured from your page") · structured observations, including absence proof for missing-element findings ("3 pages searched — not found") Per-scan evidence store, version-stamped
Hard constraint — citations are rendered, never generated: the LLM maps page evidence to a rule ID at scan time; the citation text and links are looked up from the signed registry. The model never produces a legal citation at runtime. A hallucinated citation is treated as a build-breaking defect (CI check), same severity class as a language-rule violation.
Interpretive basis — the citation is an anchor, not the whole rule (per Michael 7/8): a risk is often not clearly defined in the black-letter statute; it develops in the case law and agency guidance interpreting the statute, so the bare statutory text may not apply straightforwardly. Each registry entry therefore carries an interpretive-basis field — statute / regulation / agency guidance / case law & enforcement — and where the operative rule lives in interpretation rather than in the text, the citation points to the controlling guidance or enforcement synthesis (not just the bare section) and the "Relevant rules and context" narrative carries the interpretive gloss. This classification is reviewable per-rule in the citation registry (§9) and is part of what counsel signs.
  • Temporal anchor: citations carry no individual dates. One block-level line — "Rule corpus v2026.06 · reference only — how these rules apply to you depends on facts a scan can't assess" — anchors all citations to the corpus version, which is exactly the object counsel signs. Proof carries the scan date separately: "rules as of corpus date, your site as of scan date."
  • Versioned links: where the official source supports point-in-time URLs (EUR-Lex consolidated versions, eCFR date views), the registry stores the version the corpus ingested; otherwise the current official URL plus the corpus-date semantics. Link health is checked in the monthly corpus refresh (§10); a materially changed source triggers the SM C re-sign path.
  • Internal sign-off, zero external endorsement: merchants see the corpus version number only. No "counsel-reviewed" or "legal-approved" labels appear anywhere in the merchant UI — exposing counsel as named endorsers would increase reliance and UPL exposure. Sign-off records live in the internal audit log (§5.5).
  • Scope note for counsel: the source layer makes the corpus sign-off object concrete — what you sign now includes the merchant-visible citation registry. See §12 items 3, 9, 10.

14. Production architecture ⭐ (new in v1.3)

The merchant dashboard demo is a mock. This section defines how the real system is built so the legal guarantees above (citations rendered not generated, counsel-signed corpus, no runtime suppression) hold in production, not just on paper.

14.1 Three layers + an evidence store

Today rules, analysis, citations, and presentation are entangled in the demo's React source. Production separates them:

LayerResponsibilityWho owns it
① Rule & citation registry (the "law") Authoritative record. Per rule: rule_id · category · market · assertion text · citations[] (label, official URL, point-in-time URL, interpretive_basis) · scope (in/out) · status · corpus_version · sign-off record. Single source of truth — not in React code, not in Notion. Counsel-signed (SM C, §6.3)
② Rule engine / scan pipeline (the "analysis") The LLM's job is strictly bounded: given a page and the in-scope rule set from a signed corpus version, output rule_id + verdict (verified / review / not-assessable) + evidence (excerpt or observation). It never writes citations or legal conclusions. Engineering
③ Report / presentation (the "projection") Single finding entity → two projections (Overview checklist + Review Items queue, §6.4 / §8), status echo, corpus-version stamp, Source/Proof panes. Citation text is a deterministic lookup of the finding's rule_id against the registry — never model output. Engineering / Product
+ Evidence store (the "proof") Per-scan page snapshots (excerpt offsets, timestamps) + network traces. Backs the Proof layer (§5.6) and per-report provenance. Engineering
· Data flow — citations never originate in the model

14.2 Registry as version-controlled files; sign-off = an approved PR

Recommendation: store the registry as structured files (YAML/JSON) in a repository, and make counsel sign-off equal an approved pull request. One mechanism does the work of three:
  • Sign-off workflow = PR Draft → counsel review → approve. The approval record is the signature. This is State Machine C (§6.3) implemented on tooling that already exists.
  • Audit trail = git history — every citation change, who made it, who approved it, immutable. Replaces the need for a bespoke sign-off log.
  • Corpus version = a release tag (e.g. v2026.06). "In production" = the latest signed tag; the scan pipeline only ever pulls a signed tag.
  • No drift = the demo/mock and the production pipeline both read the same registry source. The demo becomes a consumer, not a second copy — this closes the three-copy drift risk flagged in §5.6.
  • Counsel review a rendered, readable diff (the current Notion registry view, generated from the files) — not raw YAML. Review venue is flexible; the single source is not.

14.3 Grounding & anti-hallucination gates (CI)

  • Grounding gate: a finding with no attached evidence, or referencing a rule_id not present in the signed corpus, is rejected — it cannot render. Build fails.
  • No-generated-citation check: confirms every citation string in output maps to a registry entry (§5.6 hard constraint) — a hallucinated citation is a build-breaking defect, same severity class as a language-rule violation.
  • Honeypot regression: every registry change runs the scan against the honeypot set (Seel demo storefront + anonymized Seel-using merchant pages + non-Seel controls) and confirms no scope-out-category finding and no banned-language output (§6.2).
  • LLM error class is bounded: the engine can mis-map evidence to a rule (a false positive/negative), caught by the 5% counsel spot-check and the N=5 new-category gate (§5.3). It can never invent law — the worst case is a missed or spurious match, never a fabricated citation.

14.4 Update lifecycle & governance

  • Triggers: statute/case-law developments · new market or category · broken/moved official links.
  • Every update → PR → counsel review → approve → new tag. Nothing reaches a merchant except through a signed tag (re-sign target ≤ 5 business days, §5.3).
  • Case-law drift (Michael 7/8): rules whose interpretive_basis = case law & enforcement are flagged for scheduled re-review — they drift fastest and the operative rule is not fixed in statutory text (§5.6).
  • Link health: monthly automated check (§10); a materially changed source triggers the re-sign path.
  • History provenance: each scan stamps the corpus version used (visible in the merchant's History tab); that version resolves to the signed tag → approval record.
What this architecture guarantees, end to end: every citation a merchant sees traces to a counsel-approved tag; the model cannot originate legal text; scope decisions are uniform and made at authoring time (no runtime suppression); and there is exactly one source of truth for what the rules are.
